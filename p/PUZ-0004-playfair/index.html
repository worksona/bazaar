Learn — letters in pairs

A keyword fills a 5×5 grid; pairs of letters move around it by three rules.

Playfair packs a keyword into a 5×5 square (I and J share a cell), then enciphers the message two letters at a time. For each pair: if they share a row, take the letter to the right of each; if they share a column, take the one below; otherwise they form a rectangle — swap to the opposite corners on the same rows. Doubled letters get an X wedged between them, and an odd final letter is padded with X.

Why it was a leap — and where it cracks

Because it hides whole pairs, single-letter counts go flat: there are 600 possible pairs, not 26 letters, so al-Kindi’s tally is useless. But Playfair still leaks. Common English pairs like TH and ER stay common in the ciphertext, a reversed pair (AB/BA) encrypts to a related pattern, and a single guessed word — a crib — pins down chunks of the square. Station 3 gives you a square-tester so you can run a keyword down and watch the message resolve.
