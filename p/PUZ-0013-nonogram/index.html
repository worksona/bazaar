Nonogram

Also called Picross. The numbers along each row and column are runs of filled cells; obey them all and a hidden picture develops out of the grid.

difficulty · intermediate⏱ 15–25 minyou’ll learn line deduction

1

Learn — numbers into a picture

Each clue lists the lengths of the filled runs in that line, in order.

A row clue of 2 2 4 means: somewhere in that row, reading left to right, sit a run of 2 filled cells, then a run of 2, then a run of 4 — with at least one gap between each run. Columns work the same, top to bottom. Satisfy every row and column clue at once and the filled cells form a picture.

“
Why it belongs here: a nonogram is two sets of constraints — rows and columns — pulling on the same cells. You never guess; you find the cell that only one arrangement can explain, and the image emerges from logic alone.
2

Technique — overlap and edges

Big runs reveal themselves before you’ve placed anything.

The overlap

A run longer than half the line must overlap itself wherever it’s pushed. In a 10-wide row, a run of 6 always fills the middle 2 cells no matter where it starts — fill those for free.

Mark the empties

Marking cells you’ve ruled out (with a ·) is half the work. A full line’s clue plus a few certain fills usually forces the gaps, which forces more fills. Alternate between rows and columns and the picture cascades.

Click a cell to cycle blank → ■ filled → · ruled-out → blank. Only filled cells count toward the picture.
