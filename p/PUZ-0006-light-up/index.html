1

Learn — four rules, no guessing

Light Up (Akari) was published by Nikoli, the house that popularised Sudoku.

You place lamps on the white squares of a grid. A lamp lights its own square and shines in straight lines — up, down, left, right — until a black wall stops it. Your job is to light the whole board while obeying four rules:

  • Every white square must be lit.
  • No lamp may shine on another lamp — two lamps can’t share a row or column with no wall between them.
  • A numbered wall touches exactly that many lamps on its four orthogonal sides.
  • A 0 wall touches no lamps at all.
“
Why it belongs here: the cipher rungs reward statistical instinct; this one rewards pure logic. Every lamp can be forced by the rules — a solved Light Up never needs a guess, only careful reading.
2

Techniques — how to force a lamp

Three deductions get you through almost any board.

The 0-wall

A 0 wall forbids lamps on all four neighbours. Mark those cells with a · so you remember they’re empty. This often leaves a nearby square with only one way to be lit.

The crowded wall

Count a wall’s open sides. A 3 with exactly three white neighbours must lamp all three; a 1 beside a single open side forces that one. Fill the forced lamps, then mark the rest of that wall’s sides empty.

The only route

If a white square can be reached by a lamp from only one direction (walls block the others), that lamp is forced. Hunt for squares hemmed in by walls — they crack the board open.

“
The marker matters: use the · state liberally. Knowing where a lamp can’t go is as powerful as knowing where one must.
