The Two-Time Pad

A cipher with perfect secrecy — provably unbreakable. Used once. The lock here isn’t the maths; it’s the operator who used the same key twice.

difficulty · advanced ⏱ 25–35 min you’ll learn secure-vs-solvable + key reuse
  1. 1 Learn
  2. 2 Encrypt / Decrypt
  3. 3 Analyze
  4. 4 Crack the challenge

New here? The cipher itself can’t be broken — so don’t try. Read top to bottom; the way in is a human mistake, and the tools below exploit it.

1

Learn — perfect, until it isn’t

The one-time pad is the only cipher proven unbreakable — under one strict rule.

A stream cipher turns a key into a long river of random-looking bytes — the keystream — and XORs it into your message. If that keystream is truly random, as long as the message, and never reused, the result is a one-time pad: Shannon proved in 1949 that the ciphertext reveals nothing. No amount of computing breaks it. This is the cabinet’s one genuinely secure lock.

The catch — and the whole puzzle

“Never reused” is doing all the work. XOR has a fatal symmetry: if two messages are encrypted with the same keystream K, then C₁ ⊕ C₂ = (P₁ ⊕ K) ⊕ (P₂ ⊕ K) = P₁ ⊕ P₂ — the key cancels out completely. You’re left with two English sentences XORed together, and that you can tease apart. Worse: guess one message and you recover K outright, unlocking every other message sent under it.

!
The lesson the whole field rests on: modern crypto isn’t usually broken by attacking the maths — it’s broken by misuse: a reused key or nonce, bad randomness, a leaky implementation. The primitive is sound; the operator isn’t. That’s the door you walk through here.
2

Encrypt & Decrypt — the stream

A key seeds the keystream; XOR hides the message. The same key brings it back.

encrypt gives hex; decrypt takes hex back to text
↻
Try it: encrypt, switch to decrypt, paste the hex back with the same key — your message returns. Use a different key and you get noise. So far, so secure. The danger is using one key on two messages.
3

Analyze — crib-dragging

When two messages share a keystream, a guessed word drags the truth out.

Pick two intercepts. Their XOR is P₁ ⊕ P₂. Slide a likely word (a crib) along it: at each position, crib ⊕ (C₁⊕C₂) shows what the other message says there. Where the result turns into readable English, you’ve found where your crib really sits — and learned a slice of both messages.

Crack the challenge

Five intercepts off the harbor wire — all sent under one lazy, reused keystream.

recover the keystream from one known line

Crib-dragging will show you that one intercept is just a typing-practice line the operator sent to test the wire — a sentence you’ll recognise the moment you see a few words of it. Reconstruct that whole line, tell the tool which intercept it is, and its keystream falls out — decrypting all five at once. One of them is the password.
